## Algorithm 2.2: Extended Orbit Computation (Dawes 2022)

**Input**: Lattice L of rank n, v₁, v₂ ∈ L ⊗ Q, Γ := O_A(L) where A ⊂ O(D(L)) is subgroup
**Conditions**: v₁ non-isotropic, v₁* indefinite, natural map O(L) → O(D(L)) surjective
**Goal**: Determine if v₁ ∼Γ v₂

**Steps**:
1. For i ∈ {1,2}:
   (a) Let αᵢ ∈ Q>0 be minimal such that wᵢ := αᵢvᵢ ∈ L
   (b) If α₁ ≠ α₂ or v₁² ≠ v₂² then return v₁ ≁Γ v₂
   (c) Let Kᵢ := wᵢ⊥ ⊂ L
   (d) For the natural inclusion ⟨wᵢ⟩ ⊕ Kᵢ ⊂ L ⊂ L* ⊂ ⟨wᵢ⟩* ⊕ Kᵢ*,
       let Hᵢ := L/⟨wᵢ⟩ ⊕ Kᵢ ⊂ D(⟨wᵢ⟩) ⊕ D(Kᵢ)
   (e) Let ιᵢ be the map ιᵢ : D(L) → D(⟨wᵢ⟩) ⊕ D(Kᵢ) mod Hᵢ

2. If K₁ ≇ K₂ then return v₁ ≁Γ v₂

3. For φ ⊕ ψ ∈ {±1} ⊕ Iso(K₁, K₂):
   If (φ ⊕ ψ)(H₁) = H₂ and ι₂⁻¹ ∘ (φ ⊕ ψ) ∘ ι₁ ∈ A then
   return v₁ ∼Γ v₂

4. Return v₁ ≁Γ v₂

**Key Technical Details**:
- Uses discriminant group computations D(L), D(⟨wᵢ⟩), D(Kᵢ)
- Checks isomorphism conditions on sublattices Kᵢ
- Verifies compatibility with subgroup A ⊂ O(D(L))

## Chicago Paper: Conjugacy Classes of Parabolic Subgroups of O+(1,9)(Z) (Lee 2022)

### Main Results on Isotropic Vector Orbits

**Lemma 1**: There are exactly two O(1,9)(Z)-orbits of primitive, isotropic vectors in Z^{1,9}. They are the orbits of v := H - e₁ and w := 3H - e₁ - ... - e₉.

**Theorem 2** (Mordell [Mor38]): There are exactly two isomorphism classes of positive-definite, unimodular lattices of rank 8; they are given by 8(1) and E₈.

**Computational Framework**:
- Uses Z-submodule W of Z^{10} with symmetric bilinear form Q of type (1,9):
  Q = diag(1,-1,...,-1)
- Considers pair (W,Q_{|W}) which may or may not be a lattice
- Focuses on maximal parabolic subgroups Γ = Stab_{O+(1,9)(Z)}(u) where u ∈ Z^{1,9} is primitive, isotropic, nontrivial vector

**Proof Technique for Lemma 1**:
- Shows v and w are not in same O(1,9)(Z)-orbit by examining restriction of Q to specific sublattices
- Uses signature calculations and definiteness properties
- Employs Mordell's classification of positive-definite unimodular lattices


### Explicit Computational Techniques (Lee 2022)

**Orbit Distinction Method**:
1. **Restriction Analysis**: For primitive isotropic vectors v, w, examine restrictions:
   - Z[w]⊥ = Z[H - e₁ - e₂ - e₃, e₁ - e₂, ..., e₈ - e₉] ≅ Z⁹
   - Z[v]⊥ = Z[v, e₂, e₃, ..., e₉] ≅ Z⁹

2. **Parity and Unimodularity Testing**:
   - For g ∈ O(1,9)(Z), restriction of Q to g(Z[w]⊥) is also even
   - Therefore g(w) ≠ v for all g ∈ O(1,9)(Z)
   - Check if primitive isotropic nontrivial vector u ∈ Z^{1,9} lies in O(1,9)(Z)-orbit of v or w

3. **Automorphism Construction**:
   - By unimodularity of Q, there exists u* ∈ Z^{1,9} such that Q(u,u*) = 1
   - Restriction of Q to Z[u,u*]⊥ is unimodular and indefinite
   - Restriction of Q to Z[u,u*]⊥ is unimodular and negative definite of rank 8

4. **Case Analysis by Parity**:
   - **Case 1**: If Z[u,u*]⊥ ≅ -E₈, then Q restricted to Z[u,u*]⊥ must be odd
   - **Case 2**: If Z[u,u*]⊥ ≅ 8(-1), then depending on parity of Q|_{Z[u,u*]⊥}:
     - Z[u,u*] ≅ (1) ⊕ (-1) or Z[u,u*] ≅ (Z² (0 1; 1 0))

5. **Automorphism Verification**:
   - There exists automorphism A ∈ O(1,9)(Z) such that:
     - A(Z[u,u*]) = L ≅ (1) ⊕ (-1), A(Z[u,u*]⊥) = L⊥ ≅ -E₈
     - A(Z[u,u*]) = Z[H - e₁], A(Z[u,u*]⊥) = Z[e₂,...,e₉]
   - This gives A(u) = w = 3H - e₁ - ... - e₉ ∈ L


## McConnell-MacPherson Paper: Computing Hecke Operators for Arithmetic Subgroups (2020)

### Computational Framework for Arithmetic Subgroups

**Well-Rounded Retract Method**:
- Uses well-rounded forms Z with linearly independent vectors of length 1
- Constructs Klein model with positive definite quadratic form on R²
- Divides Z by homothety to make trace 1, creating open circular disc in ab-plane
- Forms tree W in X as Γ-equivariant strong deformation retract

**Hecke Operator Computation**:
- For positive integer m, Hecke correspondence Tₘ is one-to-many map carrying lattice L to sublattices of index m
- Let Γ ⊂ Γ₀ be subgroup of finite index
- Correspondence Tₘ acts on cohomology H*(Γ\X)
- Action denoted Tₘ is a Hecke operator

**Lattice-Based Approach**:
- Understands Z in terms of lattices: X = G/K where K is subgroup of orthogonal matrices

**Technical Implementation**:

## EPFL Paper: Locally Symmetric Varieties - Neat Arithmetic Subgroups

### Section 1.3.1: Neat Subgroups - Computational Framework

**Definition of Neat Subgroups**:
If V is a finite-dimensional Q-vector space then an element α ∈ GL(V)(Q) is called **neat** if it eigenvalues generate a torsion-free subgroup of C×. If α is neat then clearly α is not torsion. An element g ∈ G(Q) is neat if ρ(g) is neat for some faithful representation ρ : G → GL(V) (in this case, it will be neat for any representation). A subgroup of G(Q) is neat if every element of that subgroup is neat.

**Computational Significance**:
Neat arithmetic subgroups are important to make sure we are getting smooth quotients. We first show some basic results (due to Borel) on the existence of those (since the arguments are interesting on their own).

**Proposition 1.3**: For any Q-group G and an arithmetic subgroup Γ ⊂ G(Q), then there exists a neat subgroup Γ' ⊂ Γ of finite index.

**Proof Method**:
- Identify G(Q) with a Q-subgroup of GLₙ(Q) via a morphism that sends Γ to a subgroup of GLₙ(Z)
- It suffices to prove the statement for Γ = GLₙ(Z)
- Let S be the set of all cyclotomic polynomials different from Φ₁(X) = X - 1 of degree at most n
- Choose a prime p such that p ∤ ∏ f(1)
                                    f∈S
- Consider the congruence subgroup Γ' = {g ∈ GLₙ(Z) : g ≡ 1 mod p}
- We claim that Γ' is neat

**Verification Process**:
If not, there exists g ∈ Γ' and s that is in the subgroup generated by the eigenvalues λ₁,...,λₙ of g such that s is a root of unity. Want to show that s = 1.

**Lemma 1.4**: The principal congruence subgroup Γ(N) ⊂ GLₙ(Q) is torsion-free for N ≥ 3.

**Technical Implementation**:
- Uses cyclotomic polynomials and their properties
- Employs congruence conditions modulo primes
- Verifies torsion-free property through eigenvalue analysis
- Ensures smooth quotient construction for locally symmetric varieties


## Comprehensive Analysis of Computational Techniques

### Classification of Algorithmic Approaches

The literature reveals several distinct computational approaches for computing orbits of isotropic vectors under arithmetic subgroups of orthogonal groups. These can be classified into the following categories:

#### 1. Direct Orbit Computation Algorithms

**Algorithm 2.1 (Dawes 2022)** provides the most comprehensive approach for non-isotropic vectors, which serves as a foundation for isotropic cases. The algorithm employs a systematic approach using discriminant group computations and isomorphism testing. The key insight is the use of invariants under O(L) as preliminary tests before attempting more expensive computations.

The algorithm begins by computing minimal scaling factors αᵢ ∈ Q>0 such that αᵢvᵢ ∈ L for input vectors vᵢ. This normalization step is crucial as it ensures that subsequent computations work with lattice elements rather than rational vectors. The algorithm then constructs orthogonal complements Kᵢ := wᵢ⊥ ⊂ L using Smith normal form decomposition, where wᵢ := αᵢvᵢ.

The discriminant group approach is particularly sophisticated. For each sublattice Kᵢ, the algorithm computes the discriminant group D(Kᵢ) and constructs natural homomorphisms pSᵢ : Hᵢ → D(Sᵢ) and pKᵢ : Hᵢ → D(Kᵢ), where Hᵢ = L/(Sᵢ ⊕ Kᵢ) ⊂ D(Sᵢ) ⊕ D(Kᵢ). This construction allows the algorithm to work with finite groups rather than infinite lattices, significantly reducing computational complexity.

#### 2. Tits' Building Construction Methods

**Algorithm 3.1 (Dawes 2022)** represents a breakthrough in computational approaches to isotropic vector orbits through the construction of Tits' buildings. The algorithm works with groups G₁ ⊂ G₂ ⊂ O*(L ⊗ Q) and systematically constructs the building B(G₁) = (N₁, E₁) where N₁ = P₁ ∪ C₁ represents orbits of totally isotropic subspaces.

The computational framework begins by establishing transversals G := {gᵢ} for G₂/G₁. For each equivalence class [E] ∈ N₂, the algorithm constructs a set H of representatives for G/∼ where the equivalence relation is defined through stabilizer subgroups. Specifically, if Lᵧ is a transversal for StabG₂(gᵧE)/StabG₂(gᵧE), then gᵧ ∼ gⱼ if and only if [gᵧgⱼ⁻¹] ∈ G₁ for lᵧ ∈ Lᵧ.

The edge construction in step 4 of Algorithm 3.1 is particularly intricate. For each pair ([ℓ], [Π]) ∈ P₁ × C₁, the algorithm constructs a finite set X of representatives for all lines in Π up to equivalence in StabG₂(Π). The restriction homomorphism ρ : StabG₂(Π) → GL(Π) plays a crucial role, and the algorithm carefully manages the finite quotient |ρ(Γ) : Im(ρ)| to ensure computational tractability.

#### 3. Maximal Lattice Specialization

**Algorithm 3.2 (Dawes 2022)** provides a specialized approach for maximal lattices of signature (2,n). This algorithm exploits the special structure of maximal lattices to achieve significant computational efficiencies. The key insight is that for maximal lattices L', the building B(O*(L')) has a particularly simple structure that can be computed directly.

The algorithm begins by selecting a primitive isotropic vector c ∈ L' and initializing P := {c}. The genus computation gen(0,n-2,D(L)) provides all isomorphism classes of lattices with the appropriate discriminant form. For each class L₀ in this genus, the algorithm constructs the Z-basis S(L₀) = {xᵢ}ᵢ₌₁²⁺ⁿ and defines the totally isotropic plane Π := ⟨x₁,x₂⟩.

The theoretical foundation relies on the fact that totally isotropic sublattices E₁*, E₂ ⊂ L' belong to the same O*(L')-orbit if and only if E₁*/E₁ ≅ E₂*/E₂. This equivalence can be checked computationally using standard lattice isomorphism algorithms, making the approach practical for implementation.

#### 4. Stabilizer Group Computations

The computation of stabilizer groups forms a critical component of all orbit algorithms. **Lemma 3.7 (Dawes 2022)** provides explicit formulas for computing StabO*(L')(ℓ) when L' is a maximal lattice and ℓ is an isotropic line. The stabilizer is given by StabO*(L')(ℓ) = ⟨t(x₁,v), O*(L'₀) | v ∈ L'₀⟩, where t(x₁,v) represents Eichler transvections.

**Eichler transvections** are defined by the formula t(e,a) : v ↦ v - ⟨v,e⟩e + ½⟨a,e⟩e, where v ∈ L. These transformations preserve the quadratic form and generate important subgroups of the orthogonal group. The computational challenge lies in efficiently representing and manipulating these transvections in matrix form.

**Lemma 3.8 (Dawes 2022)** extends this to totally isotropic planes, providing the formula StabO*(L')(Π) = ⟨t(x₁,x₄), t(x₂,x₃), t(x₁,x₃), t(x₁,v), t(x₂,v), O*(L'₀) | v ∈ L'₀⟩. The complexity of these stabilizer computations grows with the dimension of the lattice, but the explicit formulas make implementation feasible.

### Neat Arithmetic Subgroup Computational Techniques

The computational treatment of neat arithmetic subgroups requires specialized techniques that exploit their torsion-free property and finite index structure. The **EPFL framework** provides the theoretical foundation through Proposition 1.3, which guarantees the existence of neat subgroups of finite index within any arithmetic subgroup.

#### Construction of Neat Subgroups

The **congruence subgroup method** represents the most practical approach for constructing neat subgroups computationally. Given an arithmetic subgroup Γ ⊂ GLₙ(Z), the algorithm constructs a neat subgroup Γ' by selecting an appropriate prime p and defining Γ' = {g ∈ GLₙ(Z) : g ≡ 1 mod p}. The choice of prime p must satisfy the condition p ∤ ∏f∈S f(1), where S is the set of all cyclotomic polynomials different from Φ₁(X) = X - 1 of degree at most n.

The **verification process** for neatness involves checking that no non-trivial roots of unity appear among the eigenvalues of group elements. This is accomplished by analyzing the characteristic polynomials of group elements and applying results from algebraic number theory about cyclotomic polynomials. The computational challenge lies in efficiently testing the eigenvalue conditions without explicitly computing all eigenvalues.

**Lemma 1.4** provides a crucial computational shortcut by establishing that principal congruence subgroups Γ(N) ⊂ GLₙ(Q) are torsion-free for N ≥ 3. This result allows algorithms to work directly with congruence subgroups without additional verification steps, significantly simplifying implementations.

#### Orbit Computation for Neat Subgroups

The **modified Algorithm 3.1** for neat arithmetic subgroups exploits the torsion-free property to simplify stabilizer computations. Since neat subgroups contain no non-trivial torsion elements, the stabilizer subgroups StabΓ(v) for isotropic vectors v have simpler structure than in the general case. This simplification reduces the complexity of the transversal computations in step 3 of the algorithm.

The **smooth quotient property** of neat subgroups provides additional computational advantages. The quotient spaces Γ\X are smooth manifolds, allowing the use of differential geometric techniques in conjunction with algebraic methods. This hybrid approach can provide more efficient algorithms for certain classes of problems.

**Hecke operator computations** for neat arithmetic subgroups benefit from the well-rounded retract construction described in the McConnell-MacPherson framework. The torsion-free property ensures that the retract construction produces genuine manifolds rather than orbifolds, simplifying the topological computations required for Hecke operator algorithms.

#### The O(1,9)(Z) Case Study

The **Chicago paper analysis** provides the most detailed computational example available in the literature. The classification of O(1,9)(Z)-orbits of primitive isotropic vectors demonstrates the practical application of orbit computation algorithms to specific cases.

The **restriction method** employed in this case study provides a template for similar computations. For primitive isotropic vectors v and w, the algorithm examines the restrictions of the quadratic form Q to the orthogonal complements Z[v]⊥ and Z[w]⊥. The key computational steps involve:

1. **Sublattice construction**: Computing Z[v]⊥ = Z[H - e₁ - e₂ - e₃, e₁ - e₂, ..., e₈ - e₉] and verifying its rank and structure.

2. **Unimodularity testing**: Verifying that the restriction of Q to these sublattices yields unimodular lattices, which can be checked by computing discriminants.

3. **Definiteness analysis**: Determining whether the restricted quadratic forms are positive definite, negative definite, or indefinite through eigenvalue computations or Sylvester's criterion.

4. **Classification comparison**: Using Mordell's classification of positive-definite unimodular lattices of rank 8 to distinguish between the cases Z[v]⊥ ≅ -E₈ and Z[w]⊥ ≅ 8(-1).

The **automorphism construction** phase involves explicitly constructing elements A ∈ O(1,9)(Z) that map between the different orbit representatives. This requires solving systems of linear equations over the integers while preserving the quadratic form structure.

#### Discriminant Group Methods
