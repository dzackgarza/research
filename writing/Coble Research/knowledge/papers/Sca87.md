ABSTRACT. This paper is concerned with the problem of describing compact moduli spaces for algebraic K3 surfaces of given degree 2k. We mainly consider Baily-Borel's compactification of period space, in which only zero- and one-dimensional boundary components are added.
We discuss its geometric interpretation and translate the arithmetic problems that arise in describing the boundary into lattice-theoretical terms.
As a result of a partial classification of primitive isotropic sublattices of certain lattices of signature (2,19), we are then able to determine the number of zero-dimensional boundary components and, under some arithmetic constraints on k, we are able to count and describe the one-dimensional components and their configuration.

INTRODUCTION

The period space for K3 surfaces with a primitive polarization of degree 2k is of the form $\mathsf{D}_{\mathbf{k}} / \mathsf{T}_{\mathbf{k}}$ where $\mathsf{D}_{\mathbf{k}}$ is a 19-dimensional bounded symmetric domain of type IV and $\mathsf{T}_{\mathbf{k}}$ is an arithmetic group acting on $\mathsf{D}_{\mathbf{k}}$ . The global Torelli theorem for algebraic K3 surfaces, due to Piatetskii-Shapiro and Shafarevich [29], asserts that $\mathsf{D}_{\mathbf{k}} / \mathsf{T}_{\mathbf{k}}$ is in fact a coarse moduli space for K3 surfaces of degree 2k.

The objective of this paper is to describe suitable compactifications of $\mathsf{D}_{\mathbf{k}} / \mathsf{T}_{\mathbf{k}}$ . To this end, we start by describing in §1.2 what one might expect, or wish, to find at the boundary of moduli space, in terms of the Kulikov-Persson-Pinkham classification of one-variable degenerations of K3 surfaces (and some combinatorial refinements of it).

Next we consider Baily-Borel's compactification $\overline{\mathsf{D}_{\mathbf{k}} / \mathsf{T}_{\mathbf{k}}}$ , which can be viewed as the minimal "geometrically meaningful" compactification.
The description given in §2.1 is focused on the arithmetic problems (how to describe the configuration of the boundary, which only contains zero- and one-dimensional components), ignoring the global aspects of realizing $\overline{\mathsf{D}_{\mathbf{k}} / \mathsf{T}_{\mathbf{k}}}$ as a projective variety via automorphic forms.
The arithmetic problems are easily translated into lattice-theoretical terms: if $\mathsf{L}_{\mathbf{k}}$ is the primitive cohomology of a polarized K3 surface, there is a correspondence between zero- (resp.
one-) dimensional boundary components of $\overline{\mathsf{D}_{\mathbf{k}} / \mathsf{T}_{\mathbf{k}}}$ and $\mathsf{T}_{\mathbf{k}}$ -equivalence classes of isotropic lines (resp.
planes) in $\mathsf{L}_{\mathbf{k}}$ .

The geometric interpretation of the boundary of $\overline{\mathsf{D}_{\mathbf{k}} / \mathsf{T}_{\mathbf{k}}}$ is discussed in §2.2, where we indicate how points and curves in the boundary essentially correspond to different kinds of degenerations.
We also note that the Baily-Borel compactification is very "small" and the geometric information it contains is not sufficient to, say, reconstruct a birationally unique degeneration from a given arc in

§1. K3 surfaces

## 1.1 The period map.

A compact connected complex-analytic surface $X$ is called a **K3 surface** if it is regular ($q = \dim H^1(X, \mathcal{O}) = 0$) and its canonical bundle is trivial.
It is well-known (see for example [20]) that the second integral cohomology group of a K3 surface, endowed with the bilinear symmetric form given by cup product, is an even unimodular lattice of signature (3,19) and is unique up to isomorphism.
Thus (see also §3.1 below for the notation), there is an isometric isomorphism of $H^2(X; \mathbb{Z})$ with the lattice $\Lambda = H^3 \oplus E_8^2$.
We call $\Lambda$ the **K3 lattice**.

The period map for K3 surfaces can be defined as follows.
A K3 surface $X$ has a nonvanishing holomorphic 2-form, unique up to scalar multiples: its cohomology class $\omega = \omega_X$ spans the line $H^{2,0}(X) \subset H^2(X; \mathbb{C}) = \Lambda_{\mathbb{C}}$.
Moreover, $\omega$ satisfies the (Hodge-Riemann) relations $(\omega, \omega) = 0$, $(\omega, \overline{\omega}) &gt; 0$.
Thus, if $X$ is a K3 surface, we can find an isometry $\phi: H^2(X; \mathbb{Z}) \to \Lambda$ and define the **period** of $X$ (depending on the marking $\phi$) to be the point determined by $\phi(\omega_X)$ in

$$
\bar{\Omega} = \left\{ z \in \mathbb{P}(\Lambda_{\mathbb{C}}): (z, z) = 0, (z, \bar{z}) &gt; 0 \right\}.
$$

More generally, a consistent choice of markings in a smooth family $\{X_t\} \to \Delta$ of K3 surfaces determines a period map $\Delta \to \bar{\Omega}$, which associates to $t \in \Delta$ the period of $X_t$.
The local Torelli theorem for K3 surfaces, due to Kodaira, asserts that the periods give local moduli for small deformations.

In order to obtain the global version of the Torelli theorem one has to consider polarized K3 surfaces.

The global Torelli theorem ([29], [11]) states that $\Omega(h)/\Gamma(h)$ is a coarse moduli space for primitively polarized K3 surfaces of degree $2k$.
It is possible of course to avoid any reference to the vector $h \in \Lambda$: We pointed out that any two choices for $h$ are equivalent modulo isometries of $\Lambda$.
Hence the isomorphism class of the lattice $\{h\}^1 \subset \Lambda$ only depends on the integer $k$, and an obvious choice of $h$ shows that $\{h\}^1 \cong \langle -2k \rangle \otimes H^2 \otimes E_B^2$.

L_k = \langle -2k \rangle \otimes H^2 \otimes E_B^2 \tag{1.1.1}

and will be called the *primitively polarized K3 lattice of degree $2k$*. The period domain $\Omega(h)$ can be identified to

$$
\Omega_k = \{ z \in \mathbb{P}(L_k \otimes \mathbb{C}) : (z, z) = 0, (z, \overline{z}) &gt; 0 \}. \tag{1.1.2}

$$
\Omega(h)/\Gamma(h) = \Omega_{k}/\overline{O}(L_{k}) = D_{k}/\Gamma_{k}.
$$

The theory of compactifications for arithmetic quotients of symmetric bounded domains is extremely rich.
First of all there is the canonical compactification, due to Baily and Borel [3], whose explicit construction in the case of $D_k/\Gamma_k$ constitutes the main object of our attention.
We will recall the general construction of the extension $D/\Gamma \subset \overline{D/\Gamma}$, and show how we can obtain the (set-theoretical) description of the boundary $\overline{D_k/\Gamma_k} - D_k/\Gamma_k$ from a study of lattices and quadratic forms.
The disadvantages of the Baily-Borel compactification are well-known: the boundary has large codimension (in the case of $D_k/\Gamma_k$ we only add points and curves) and contains only partial geometric information (the boundary does not provide suitable moduli for degenerate K3 surfaces: we will return to this point in §2.2). On the other hand, the Baily-Borel compactification

is minimal with respect to extendability of period maps $(\Delta - \{0\})^r + D / r$ across the punctures, and in particular it is dominated by any other geometric compactification.

The second general answer to the problem of constructing a suitable compactification of $D / r$ is given by Mumford's toroidal compactification ([1], [23]). The boundary is now a divisor, with well-understood singularities, and it can incorporate deeper geometric information (see for example the partial compactification obtained by Friedman in [14, Ch. Mumford's compactification is not unique, however, involving the choice of compatible "admissible polyhedral decompositions" for cones associated to each boundary component, and its construction requires more complicated tools.
We will avoid describing the general set-up and limit ourselves to indicate, in §2.2, how it might be possible to make use in this context of certain results of Vinberg.

In order to put the description of these compactifications in the proper perspective, we now recall the principal results on the geometry of degenerations of K3 surfaces (a more detailed account can be found in [12, Ch. I]).

## 1.2 Degenerations of K3 surfaces.

We only consider, for simplicity, one-variable degenerations: these are flat proper maps $p \colon X \to \Delta$ from a smooth threefold $X$ to the unit disk $\Delta \subset C$, whose fibers $X_t = p^{-1}(t)$ are smooth K3 surfaces for all $t \neq 0$.
Thus we can think of $\Delta^* = \Delta - \{0\}$ as an arc in moduli space that is (possibly) approaching the boundary.
In order to relate the study of degenerations to compactifications of moduli spaces, it is natural to consider two degenerations $p \colon X \to \Delta$ and $p' \colon X' \to \Delta$ equivalent, when $X'$ is birational to $X$ and isomorphic to it over $\Delta^*$.
An interesting problem is then to

determine all equivalent models of a given degeneration; in particular, it is desirable to find "nice models" among the possible birational types of $p \colon X + \Delta$ (which can be useful to carry out explicit computations at the boundary of moduli space -- see Friedman's report in [14]) and criteria to decide when are two given degenerations equivalent.

A major role in the classification is played by two important theorems that apply to degenerations of K3 surfaces and allow us to make the following two basic assumptions: first, it is convenient for technical reasons to assume that the degeneration $p \colon X + \Delta$ is semistable (i.e. the central fiber $X_0$ is reduced and has only normal crossing singularities); this can be achieved, by Mumford's "semistable reduction theorem", if we allow base change operations (we modify the family $X + \Delta$ by pulling it back over the base change $\Delta + \Delta$ given by $t + t^n$ ). By a theorem of Kulikov [19] and Persson-Pinkham [27] we may then further assume that the canonical bundle of the total space $X$ is trivial.

Thus we can restrict our attention to semistable degenerations $p \colon X + \Delta$ with $K_X \equiv 0$ , or Kulikov degenerations for short.
The central fibers $X_0$ of such degenerations are easily described [19]: they can be divided into three types according to the complexity of their singularities, namely

Type I if smooth;

Type II if containing double curves: $X_0$ is a chain of elliptic ruled surfaces, with rational surfaces on either end; all double curves are (isomorphic) elliptic curves;

Type III if also having triple points: all components of $X_0$ are rational surfaces; the double curves on each component form a cycle of rational curves and the dual graph of $X_0$ is a triangulation of $S^2$ .

Moreover, these three types are distinguished by the monodromy $N = \text{Log } T$ of the degeneration (T denotes the Picard-Lefschetz transformation on $H^2(X_t; \mathbb{Z})$ obtained by transporting cycles around the origin): $N = 0$ for Type I, $N^2 = 0$, $N \neq 0$ for Type II and $N^3 = 0$, $N^2 \neq 0$ for Type III.

Kulikov degenerations thus obviously represent "nice models" for semistable degenerations of K3 surfaces, and indeed they provide the point of departure for the construction of partial compactifications of moduli spaces for polarized K3 surfaces as described in [11], obtained by identifying certain "stable" models for degenerations of Type II in the presence of a polarization.

Returning to the non-polarized case, it is easily seen that a given degeneration admits many distinct Kulikov models; in looking for better birational forms among such models, Miranda and Morrison [12, Ch.V] discovered an algorithmic procedure by which, using certain elementary birational modifications, one can obtain a normal form for the singular fiber, the so-called minus-one-form (this is already very convenient for calculations, but minus-one-forms are still not unique).

In a joint work with Friedman [13] a combinatorial classification of Kulikov degenerations was obtained, based on a different approach; this consisted of looking for arithmetic invariants of the monodromy of the degeneration.
The result of the classification is that the combinatorics is completely reflected in the arithmetic of $N$: two degenerations are equivalent precisely when their monodromy matrices are conjugated by $O(A)$.
The matrix $N$ in turn is determined by the value of two numerical invariants that admit a simple geometric interpretation.
These arithmetic features will be described in more detail in §2.2, where we discuss their relation to the boundary components of possible compactifications of moduli space.

# §2.

Compactifications

In this section we look in detail at the Baily-Borel compactification of moduli space $D_k / \Gamma_k$.
We sketch the general construction, valid for arithmetic quotients of symmetric bounded domains, concentrating on the description of the boundary components.
In the case of $D_k / \Gamma_k$, the boundary components are in one-to-one correspondence with $\Gamma_k$-equivalence classes of isotropic sublattices of the polarized K3 lattice $L_k$ (1.1.1). This correspondence can be described explicitly and all questions about the structure of the boundary are converted into lattice-theoretical problems.

We conclude by discussing what geometric information is contained in this compactification and what attempts might be made to construct larger compactifications.

## 2.1 Baily-Borel compactification.

We start by recalling the description of a canonical compactification, due to Baily and Borel [3], of the quotient of a symmetric bounded domain $D$ by a discrete arithmetically defined group $\Gamma$ of automorphisms of $D$.
We can assume equivalently that we are given a connected linear algebraic group $G$, defined and simple over $\mathbb{Q}$, with associated irreducible symmetric space $D = G_{\mathbb{R}} / K$, where $K$ is a maximal compact subgroup of the group $G_{\mathbb{R}}$ of real points of $G$.
The subgroup $\Gamma \subset G$ will be assumed to be arithmetic, that is, contained in $G_{\mathbb{Q}}$ and commensurable with $G_{\mathbb{Z}}$.

The first step toward the construction of the Baily-Borel compactification $\overline{D / \Gamma}$ of $D / \Gamma$ consists in giving the set-theoretical description of $\overline{D / \Gamma}$.
To this purpose one introduces an auxiliary space $D^*$, consisting of the union of $D$ and certain components of its boundary, the so-called rational boundary components; here, and in general when referring to the boundary of $D$, we think of $D$ as

FRANCESCO SCATTONE

contained in its compact dual $\overline{D}$, for example via the Borel embedding; thus we have $D \subset D^{*} \subset \overline{D}$.
The group $\Gamma$ still operates on $D^{*}$, and $\overline{D / \Gamma}$ is defined to be the quotient space $D^{*} / \Gamma$.

Before giving the precise definition of $D^{*}$, let's look at the simplest example, where $D$ is the unit disk.
A convenient realization of $D$ is then as the upper half plane $H$ contained in $\overline{D} = P^1$.
As our arithmetic group $\Gamma$ we can take $\Gamma = \mathfrak{SL}(2;Z)$ or, more generally, any Fuchsian group of the first kind $\Gamma \subset GL^{+}(2;Q)$.
Let $H^{*} = H \cup Q \cup \{= \}$ denote the union of $H$, the rational points on the real axis and the point $=$, in other words the extended upper half plane obtained by adjoining the cusps of $\Gamma$, and give $H^{*}$ the horocycle topology: neighborhoods of $z \in H$ are the usual ones, a basis of neighborhoods of $z = =$ is obtained by taking all sets of the form $= \cup \{z \in H : \text{Im } z &gt; a &gt; 0\}$, and a basis of neighborhoods of $z \in Q$ consists of all $z \cup \{$ interior of a circle tangent to the real axis at $z \}$.

## 2.2 Arithmetic of degenerations.

We now want to explain briefly the connection between the arithmetic aspects of one-parameter degenerations of K3 surfaces and the arithmetic description of the boundary components of the Baily-Borel compactification of $\mathsf{D}_{\mathbf{k}} / \Gamma_{\mathbf{k}}$ .

Let $p \colon X + \Delta$ be a Kulikov degeneration.
Select a point $t \in \Delta - \{0\}$ and let $T \in O(H^2(X_t; Z)) = O(\Delta)$ denote the Picard-Lefschetz transformation.

## 3.1 Lattices.

A *lattice* is a finitely generated free abelian group $L$ together with a symmetric bilinear form $b: L \times L \to \mathbb{Z}$, which we also denote by $(, )$.
If $B = (b_{ij})$ is an $n \times n$ integral symmetric matrix, we use the symbol $\langle B \rangle$ to denote the lattice with basis $\{v_1, \ldots, v_n\}$ and bilinear form $(v_i, v_j) = b_{ij}$.

An *isomorphism* of lattices will always mean a group isomorphism that is compatible with the bilinear form.
Thus, two lattices $\langle B \rangle$ and $\langle B' \rangle$ are isomorphic if and only if $B' = {}^tABA$ for some $A \in GL(n; \mathbb{Z})$.
The *orthogonal group* of $L$, denoted by $O(L)$, is by definition the group of all automorphisms of $L$.

The *quadratic* form associated to $L$ is the function $q: L \to \mathbb{Z}$ defined by $q(v) = (v, v)$.
We say that $L$ is an *even* lattice if its quadratic form takes even values; equivalently, if $B$ is the matrix defined by a basis of $L$, we require that all the diagonal entries of $B$ are even.

A lattice $L$ is called *positive* (resp.
*negative*) *definite* if $q(v) &gt; 0$ (resp.
$&lt; 0$) for all $v \in L - \{0\}$; and *indefinite* otherwise.
The signature of $L$ is the pair $(n_+, n_−)$ where $n_+$ (resp.
$n_−$) is the maximum rank of positive (resp.
negative) definite sublattices of $L$.
We say that $L$ is *nondegenerate* when $\text{rk } L = n_+ + n_−$, and in this case the minimum of $n_+, n_−$ is called the *index* of $L$.

We define the **discriminant** of a lattice $L$ to be the number $d(L) = |\det((v_i, v_j))|$, where $\{v_1, \ldots, v_n\}$ is an arbitrary basis of $L$.
$L$ is nondegenerate exactly when $d(L) \neq 0$ and it is called **unimodular** when $d(L) = 1$.
The signature $(n_+, n_-)$ of an even unimodular lattice necessarily satisfies the condition $n_+ \equiv n_- \pmod{8}$.

The two basic examples of even unimodular lattices are given by the **hyperbolic plane** $H = \langle ( \begin{array}{cc} 0 &amp; 1 \\ 1 &amp; 0 \end{array} ) \rangle$ and the root lattice $E_8$: with a slight deviation from the general convention we shall denote by $E_8$ the lattice $\mathbf{Z}^8$ with bilinear form given by the negative of the Cartan matrix of the root system $E_8$.
Thus $E_8$ is a negative definite lattice, and $-E_8$ is the positive definite analogue.

The classification of indefinite unimodular lattices is well-known and very simple (see [31]); in particular, every even indefinite unimodular lattice can be realized as an orthogonal sum of copies of $H$, $E_8$ and $-E_8$ in essentially a unique way (the only relation being $E_8 \oplus (-E_8) \cong H^8$).

The case of definite lattices is quite different.
Let us call a lattice **indecomposable** when it cannot be obtained as an orthogonal direct sum of two nontrivial sublattices.
Then Eichler proved that every definite lattice splits uniquely into an orthogonal sum of indecomposable lattices (see [16] for a simple proof), and Kneser [17] has determined by explicit computation all indecomposable lattices of rank $n$ and discriminant $d$ within the bounds $n + d &lt; 17$, $d &lt; 3$; such calculations however are not suitable for larger values of $n + d$ since the number of distinct classes grows very rapidly.
For every unimodular definite lattices, whose rank must be divisible by 8, structure theorems have only been obtained when the rank is equal to 8, 16 or 24 (giving respectively 1, 2 and 24 distinct isomorphism classes of such lattices).
The explicit description of these three cases will be discussed in §3.5.

## 3.2 Discriminant-quadratic forms.

We recall in this paragraph the terminology and some relevant results from Nikulin [25] concerning the so-called discriminant-quadratic form associated to an even lattice.
Since the discussion applies to nondegenerate lattices only, we shall use for short the term "lattice" throughout this section to mean "nondegenerate lattice".

A quadratic form $q$ on a finite abelian group $G$ is a map $q: G \to \mathbb{Q}/2\mathbb{Z}$ that satisfies:

(1) $q(\mathbf{a}\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{a}^2\mathbf{q}(\mathbf{x})$, for all $\mathbf{x} \in G$, $\mathbf{a} \in \mathbb{Z}$

(2) $q(x + y) \equiv q(x) + q(y) + 2b(x,y) \mod 2\mathbb{Z}$, all $x, y \in G$

where $b: G \times G \to \mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}$ is a symmetric bilinear form.
The form $q$ is called nondegenerate when $b$ is nondegenerate.

Consider now an even lattice $L$ with quadratic form $q(x) = b(x, x)$.
If $L$ is nondegenerate, then the quotient $G_L = L^*/L$ is a finite abelian group, canonically endowed with a bilinear and a quadratic form: if $\overline{b}: L^* \times L^* \to \mathbb{Q}$ is the rational extension of $b$, then $b_L: G_L \times G_L \to \mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}$ and $q_L: G_L \to \mathbb{Q}/2\mathbb{Z}$ are defined by

$$
\begin{array}{l}
b_{L}(x + L, y + L) \equiv \overline{b}(x, y) \mod \mathbb{Z} \\
q_{L}(x + L) \equiv \overline{b}(x, x) \mod 2\mathbb{Z} \\
\end{array}
$$

It is immediate to verify that $q_L$ is a quadratic form on $G_L$ in the sense specified above; following Nikulin, we call $q_L$ the discriminant(-quadratic) form of $L$.

It is known ([36, Theorem 6]) that any quadratic form on a finite group arises as the discriminant form of some even lattice.
Furthermore, two even lattices $L$ and $L'$ have isomorphic discriminant forms if and only if they are "stably equivalent", that is $L \otimes E \cong L' \otimes E'$ for some even unimodular lattices $E$, $E'$.
In particular, the signature of a finite quadratic form $q$ can be

### 3.3 Genera of lattices.

Discriminant-quadratic forms are closely related to the concept of "local equivalence" of lattices, which is of basic importance in the theory of quadratic forms.
Namely, let $\mathbb{Z}_{p}$ denote the ring of $p$-adic integers (with the convention that $\mathbb{Z}_{p} = \mathbb{R}$), and for a lattice $L$ let $L_{p} = L\oplus\mathbb{Z}_{p}$ stand for the corresponding lattice over $\mathbb{Z}_{p}$: then two lattices $L$ and $M$ are said to belong to the same genus if $L_{p} \cong M_{p}$ for every $p = 2,3,5,7,\ldots, \infty$.
The interest of this definition lies in the fact that there exists a fairly simple description of the isomorphism classes of lattices over $\mathbb{Z}_{p}$ (with some complications only for $p=2$) and that this local theory comes close to giving a complete description over $\mathbb{Z}$, since any given genus contains only finitely many isomorphism classes of (integral) lattices.
Furthermore, in terms of discriminant forms, Nikulin shows

[25, 1.9.4] that two even lattices are in the same genus if and only if they have same signature and same discriminant-quadratic form.

We shall be interested later in determining the number of isomorphism classes in certain genera of lattices.

This problem admits a simple and explicit solution in the case of indefinite lattices, where a genus "usually" consists of just one isomorphism class (see for example [8, §11.1]). Theorem 3.3.1 (Nikulin).
Let $\mathbf{L}$ be an even nondegenerate indefinite lattice with $\operatorname{rk} \mathbf{L} \ni \mathfrak{h}(\mathbf{G}_{\mathbf{L}}) + 2$ . Then:

(i) the genus of $\mathbf{L}$ contains only one class; and (ii) the canonical homomorphism $O(L) \to O(G_L)$ is surjective.

## 3.4 The Minkowski-Siegel formula.

Suppose $L$ is a definite lattice; then $O(L)$, being discrete in the compact group $O(L_R)$, is a finite group; let $o(L)$ denote its order.
The number $o(L)$ is of course an invariant of the isomorphism class of $L$, but it is not an invariant of the genus.
The *weight* $W(\mathcal{G})$ of a genus $\mathcal{G}$ of definite lattices is defined to be the number of classes $L$ in $\mathcal{G}$, each counted with multiplicity $1/o(L)$: thus, if $L_1, \ldots, L_h$ are the distinct isomorphism classes in $\mathcal{G}$,

$$
W(\mathcal{G}) = \frac{1}{o(L_1)} + \frac{1}{o(L_2)} + \ldots + \frac{1}{o(L_h)}.
$$

It turns out that there exist more or less effective methods, such as the formula we discuss below, to compute the value of $W(\mathcal{G})$ for any given genus $\mathcal{G}$, based on the knowledge of just one lattice in $\mathcal{G}$.
From that, one can obtain estimates on the number of distinct classes in a genus: indeed, the order of the automorphism group of a definite lattice of rank $n$ is at least 2, since $O(L)$ contains $\pm \mathrm{id}$, and is known [22] to be a divisor of the number $c_n$ defined by

$$
c_n = \prod_{\mathfrak{p} \text{ prime}} \mathfrak{p}^{\mathfrak{m}_{\mathfrak{p}}(n)}, \quad \mathfrak{m}_{\mathfrak{p}}(n) = \sum_{r=0}^{\infty} \left\lfloor \frac{n}{\mathfrak{p}^{r}(\mathfrak{p}-1)} \right\rfloor.
$$

Hence, if $\mathcal{G}$ is a genus of definite lattices of rank $n$, the number $h$ of distinct classes in $\mathcal{G}$ satisfies the inequality

$$
2 W(\mathcal{G}) &lt; h &lt; c_n W(\mathcal{G}). \tag{3.4.1}
$$

We remark that these bounds are in general not very satisfactory since the constants $c_n$ increase very rapidly with $n$ (for instance, $n! &lt; c_n &lt; (2n)!$), but they have the advantage of depending only on the rank $n$ and it might be possible to be more precise in specific

examples.

The Minkowski-Siegel formula, which can often be used for explicit computations, expresses the weight of a genus $\mathcal{G}$ of definite lattices as an infinite product

$$
\frac{1}{W(\mathcal{G})} = \prod_{p=2,3,\ldots,m} \alpha_p(\mathcal{G})
$$

over all primes $p$ and $p = \infty$, where the numbers $\alpha_p(\mathcal{G})$ are defined as follows (see [34] and [8, Appendix B]).

Let $L$ be any lattice in the genus $\mathcal{G}$, put $n = rkL$, $d = |\det(L)|$, and let $M$ be an $n \times n$ matrix representing the bilinear form on $L$.
Also, for any integer $q$, let $E_q(M)$ be the number of integral $n \times n$ matrices $A$ (mod $q$) that satisfy the congruence $t_{AMA} \equiv M \mod q$.
Then $\alpha_\infty$ is defined, in terms of the gamma function, by

$$
\alpha_\infty(\mathcal{G}) = \frac{\sum_{n(n+1)/4} n(n+1)/4}{2 \pi \left(\frac{1}{2}\right) \pi \left(\frac{2}{2}\right) \cdots \pi \left(\frac{n}{2}\right) d^{(n+1)/2}}
$$

and, for all finite primes $p$, $\alpha_p$ is defined by

$$
\alpha_p(\mathcal{G}) = \lim_{t \to \infty} \frac{1}{2} E_p t(M) p^{-tn(n-1)/2}
$$

## 3.5 Reflections and root lattices.

Throughout this paragraph, L will denote a nondegenerate lattice.
Every nonisotropic vector $ v \in L $ defines an involution $ R_v $ of $ L_0 $, acting as a reflection in the hyperplane orthogonal to $ v $, namely

$$
R_v \colon w + w - 2 \frac{(v, w)}{(v, v)} v.
$$

It is easily proved that the reflections generate the full rational orthogonal group $ O(L*0) $: given any $ \phi \in O(L_0) $, choose a nonisotropic vector $ v \in L $ and write $ w = \phi(v) $.
Then, if $ v-w $ is not isotropic, the reflection $ R = R*{v-w} $ carries $ w $ to $ v $ and hence $ R \cdot \phi $ fixes $ v $ and maps $ (Qv)^\perp $ to itself (if $ v-w $ is isotropic, then $ v+w $ is not and one can use $ R = R*v R*{v+w} $). By induction on the rank, the restriction of $ R \cdot \phi $ to $ (Qv)^\perp $ is a product of reflections $ R_1 R_2 \ldots R_m $ and if we extend these trivially to $ L_0 $ we obtain $ \phi = R R_1 R_2 \ldots R_m $, as desired.

A primitive vector $ v \in L $ will be called a generalized root of $ L $ if the corresponding reflection $ R_v $ is an integral automorphism, i.e. if $ R_v L = L $.
This is clearly equivalent to the condition that $ (v, v) $ divides 2 $ div(v) $, which is automatically satisfied if for example $ (v, v) $ is equal to $ \pm 1 $ or $ \pm 2 $ (the converse being true only for unimodular lattices).
We want to exclude the first case and restrict our discussion to the case of an even lattice $ L $; also, since later we shall be interested mostly in negative definite lattices, our definitions and notation are slightly different from the usual ones, but more convenient for our purposes.

We agree to call a vector $ v \in L $ minimal if $ (v, v) = -2 $.
The set of minimal vectors (or roots) of $ L $ is denoted by

$$
R(L) = \{v \in L: (v, v) = -2 \};
$$

it is contained in the set of generalized roots

$$
\hat{R}(L) = \{v \in L: v \text{ primitive}, (v, v) &lt; 0, R_v L = L \}.
$$

We denote by $\underline{L}$ the sublattice of $L$ spanned by $R(L)$: $\underline{L}$ is a negative definite even lattice and hence it splits uniquely into an orthogonal direct sum of indecomposable negative definite even lattices spanned by minimal vectors.

We stress again that since we are discussing negative definite lattices, a root lattice will mean the lattice (of appropriate rank) with bilinear form given by the negative of the Cartan matrix of the corresponding root system.

Thus $\underline{L}$ is an orthogonal sum of root lattices, and its decomposition agrees with the (unique) decomposition of $R(L)$ into irreducible root systems.
We shall call $\underline{L}$ the type of the negative definite even lattice $L$.

## 3.7 Elementary transformations.

Important examples of automorphisms of an indefinite even lattice are given by the so-called elementary transformations introduced by Eichler ([10, §3]; see also [20, §2]).

Let $L$ be an even lattice.
For any $f, x \in L$ such that $(f, f) = (f, x) = 0$, the elementary transformation $E_{f,x}$ associated to $f, x$ is defined by

$$
E_{f,x}: y \rightarrow y + (y, x) f - \frac{1}{2}(x, x)(y, f) f - (y, f) x.
$$

It is immediate to verify that each $E_{f,x}$ is an isometry of $L$.
Let $\delta(L)$ denote the subgroup of $O(L)$ generated by the elementary transformations.
It is a normal subgroup, since $\phi E_{f,x} \phi^{-1} = E_{\phi f, \phi x}$ for any $\phi \in O(L)$.
It can also be verified by inspection that for any $E \in \delta(L)$ and any $y \in L$, $E(y) \to E \circ \phi$ and therefore $E \in \overline{O}(L)$.
Moreover, it is shown in [10, Ch.1] that all elementary transformations have spinor norm 1, hence

# §4.

Primitive isotropic sublattices of rank one

We restrict our attention here to the 0-dimensional (or Type III) boundary components of the Baily-Borel compactification of the moduli space $\mathsf{D}_{\mathbf{k}} / \Gamma_{\mathbf{k}}$ of primitively polarized K3 surfaces of degree 2k.

Put $k = \overline{k} N^2$ with $\overline{k}$ squarefree and $N &gt; 0$ . The aim of this section is to prove the following

Theorem 4.0.1. The Baily-Borel compactification of $\mathsf{D}_{\mathbf{k}} / \Gamma_{\mathbf{k}}$ has exactly $[\frac{\mathsf{N} + 2}{2}]$ boundary components of Type III.

As indicated in §2.1, the proof is based on the study of $\Gamma_{k}$ -equivalence of isotropic rank one sublattices of the primitively polarized K3 lattice of degree 2k. It is possible in this case to give a complete explicit description for all values of k.

The only intrinsic property that one needs to obtain the classification is that a polarized K3 lattice contains two hyperbolic planes as direct summands; it is therefore advantageous to treat first the general case of all such lattices.
The basic principle then for classifying $\Gamma$ -equivalence classes of primitive isotropic vectors in $L$ is that any differences that cannot be detected in $G_L$ are taken care of by the group of elementary isometries (this is Prop.
3.7.3). Thus one only has to look at the distinct possibilities arising in $G_L$ , namely at the set $H = \{\text{images through } v \to v^* \text{ of all isotropic vectors of } L\}$ . ## 5.3 Second reduction.

The approach of the previous section, besides showing the role of the genus of $ E^1/E $ in the description of the orbit spaces $ I_2(L)/\Gamma $, makes it possible to obtain some results by direct matrix manipulations. $$
\text{isomorphism class of } E^1/E \leftarrow E \text{ (modulo } r_1 \right) \leftarrow C_E
$$
