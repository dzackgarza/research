Complete moduli spaces of Coble surfaces

Abstract

We describe a modular KSBA stable pair compactification of the moduli space of Coble surfaces and compare it to a semitoroidal compactification of the corresponding period domain. We further describe KSBA stable limits in terms of integral affine structures.

1 Introduction

A Coble surface is a smooth projective rational surface \(S\) with \({\left\lvert {-K_S} \right\rvert} = \emptyset\) but \({\left\lvert {-2K_S} \right\rvert}\neq \emptyset\). Such surfaces arise from the work of and on Cremona transformations of \({\mathbf{P}}^2\) preserving an irreducible rational sextic \(C\) with ten nodes – the blowup \(S\) along these nodes yields a Coble surface. shows that the Cremona class of such a curve \(C\) can be written as a union of finitely many projective equivalence classes, and that if \(C\) is sufficiently general then \(\mathop{\mathrm{Aut}}(S) \cong W(E_{10})\), the Weyl group of an infinite root system of type \(E_{10}\). Moreover, he shows that that modulo \(\mathop{\mathrm{Aut}}(S)\), there are only finitely many smooth rational negative curves on \(S\).

Coble surfaces occur as degenerations of Enriques surfaces and were ultimately classified in . As such, they are intimately tied to the theory of algebraic K3 surfaces equipped with a nonsymplectic involution, which were classified by . For a reduced plane sextic \(C\), the double cover of \(S\) branched along the proper transform of \(C\) is a K3 surface \(X\) which can be realized as a degeneration of the universal double cover of an Enriques surface, where \(X\) acquires an \(A_1\) singularity fixed by the Enriques involution. The resulting quotient has a quartic singularity whose resolution is an irreducible smooth rational curve \(\tilde C\) satisfying \(\tilde C^2 = -4\), and thus by is a Coble surface.

We will be interested in Coble surfaces in the strong sense, where \({\left\lvert {-2K_S} \right\rvert} = \left\{{C_1 + \cdots + C_n}\right\}\) is a single reduced divisor comprised of \(n\) disjoint smooth rational curves referred to as boundary components of \(S\). It is known that \(1\leq n\leq 10\), and for each such \(n\), there is a moduli space \(F_{\Co, n}\) of Coble surfaces with \(n\) boundary components. When \(n=1\), the moduli space \(F_{\Co} \mathrel{\vcenter{:}}= F_{\Co, 1}\) of Coble surfaces can be described as a boundary divisor \(\cH_{-2}\) in the 10-dimensional moduli space \(F_{\operatorname{En}}\) of unpolarized Enriques surfaces, and thus \(F_{\Co}\) is 9-dimensional. Moreover, \(F_{\Co}\) was shown to be rational in via a comparison to a moduli space of cuspidal plane quintics. A natural question is whether or not \(F_{\Co}\) admits a geometrically meaningful, modular compactification \(\overline{F_{\Co}}\), and if so, if the boundary \(\partial \overline{F_{\Co}}\) can be described and classified. Toward this end, we turn to the stable pair compactifications of Kollár, Shepherd-Barron, and Alexeev .

The search for modular compactifications of moduli spaces is a central problem in algebraic geometry. The prototypical example stems from the work of on the Deligne-Mumford-Knudsen compactification \(\overline{{ \mathcal{M}_{g, n} }}\) of the moduli space of stable pointed curves \({ \mathcal{M}_{g, n} }\). By , a similarly modular compactification \(\overline{{\mathcal{A}_g}}\) of the moduli space \({\mathcal{A}_g}\) of principally polarized abelian varieties via stable pairs exists, the normalization of which coincides with a particular choice of toroidal compactification by the work of and . By , the coarse moduli space \(F_{2d}\) of primitively polarized K3 surfaces of degree \(2d\) is isomorphic to an arithmetic quotient \(D_{L_{2d}}/\Gamma_{L_{2d}}\) of a Type IV Hermitian symmetric domain associated to a lattice \(L_{2d}\), where \(D_{L_{2d}}\) is a period domain classifying Hodge structures of K3 type and \(\Gamma_{L_{2d}}\) is an arithmetic subgroup of the orthogonal group \({\operatorname{O}}(L_{2d})\). By , such domains admit a Baily-Borel compactification which is a projective variety. For \(F_{2d}\) and related lattice-polarized moduli spaces \(F_S\) of K3 surfaces, the boundary of the compactification consists of a configuration of 0-cusps (points) and 1-cusps (curves), see . By , there additionally exist infinitely many toroidal compactifications described by a choice of fans at each cuspidal point of the Baily-Borel compactification. The work of on semitoroidal compactifications simultaneously generalizes the Baily-Borel and toroidal compactifications, retaining the advantage that the boundary can be understood in terms of semifans and studied using toric geometry.

Semitoroidal compactifications are advantageous due to their explicit and combinatorial nature, but do not a priori carry a clear modular interpretation along the boundary strata. Alternatively, letting \((S, R)\) be a pair consisting of a surface and a suitably chosen divisor, the work of yields a compactification obtained by taking the closure of pairs \((S, \varepsilon R)\) in the space of KSBA stable pairs. Although KSBA compactifications admit strong modular interpretations, their boundary strata are generally quite difficult to describe. A recent strategy employed in is to simultaneously leverage the advantages of both semitoric and KSBA compactifications by finding comparison morphisms between them. This allows the modular boundary of the KSBA compactification to be studied and classified using the combinatorics of toric geometry, lattice theory, and critically, advances in integral affine geometry and mirror symmetry.

The moduli space \(F_\Co\) of Coble surfaces with \(n=1\) boundary components admits a Hodge-theoretic period domain description of the form \(D(T_\Co)/\Gamma_\Co\) where \(T_\Co\) is a fixed lattice and \(\Gamma_\Co \mathrel{\vcenter{:}}={\operatorname{O}}(T_\Co)\) is its orthogonal group. Its Baily-Borel compactification \(\overline{F_{\Co}}^{\operatorname{BB}}\) contains only one 0-cusp \(p_0\), and thus the combinatorial data of a semitoroidal compactification is determined by a single \(\Gamma_\Co{\hbox{-}}\)invariant semifan associated to a lattice at \(p_0\). A canonical choice one can take is the Coxeter fan, formed by a fundamental domain of the action of the lattice’s Weyl group, along with its reflections. Perhaps more naturally, one can also search for a semifan \({\mathcal{F}}\) such that the resulting compactification \(\overline{F_\Co}^{{\mathcal{F}}}\) is isomorphic to the KSBA compactification \(\overline{F_\Co}\) for a suitably chosen divisor, and indeed this is what we do in this paper. To this end, we prove the following:

There is a semifan \({\mathcal{F}}\) such that there exists a morphism \begin{align*} \Psi: (\overline{F_\Co})^{\nu} \to \overline{F_\Co}^{{\mathcal{F}}} \end{align*} from the normalization of the KSBA compactification to the semitoroidal compactification associated with \({\mathcal{F}}\). The Coxeter fan of \(T_{\Co}\) is a refinement of \({\mathcal{F}}\), and stable Coble surfaces in the boundary of \(\overline{F_\Co}\) admit explicit descriptions in terms of surfaces associated to sub-Dynkin diagrams of Coxeter diagrams.

This result is made possible by recent advances in on compactifications of K3 surfaces with nonsymplectic automorphisms, along with the theory of recognizable divisors developed in . We also critically leverage the related stable pair compactification of the moduli spaces of Enriques surfaces studied in . In particular, we use the folding theory of Coxeter diagrams, their associated integral affine structures, and the theory of \(ADE+BC\) surfaces in order to explicitly describe stable degenerations of Coble surfaces.

2 Coble surfaces

2.1 The geometry of Coble surfaces

For the general theory of Coble surfaces, we refer to , along with . Following , a is a smooth projective rational surface \(S\) with \({\left\lvert {-K_S} \right\rvert} = \emptyset\) but \({\left\lvert {-2K_S} \right\rvert} \neq \emptyset\). We say \(S\) is of K3 type if \({\left\lvert {-2K_S} \right\rvert}\) consists of a single smooth divisor \(C = C_1 + \cdots + C_n\), the union of \(n\) disjoint smooth rational curves satisfying \(C_i\cdot C_j = -4\delta_{ij}\). For Coble surfaces obtained as the blowup along ten nodes of a nodal plane sextic, this calculation follows from adjunction and the genus formula. We refer to \(C\) as the anti-bicanonical curve of \(S\), and note that \(K_S\cdot C_i = -2\). The \(C_i\) are referred to as the boundary components of \(S\).

By , such a Coble surface is known to be a basic rational surface, i.e. there is a birational morphism \(\pi: S\to {\mathbf{P}}^2\). Writing \(-K_S^2 = n\), one can decompose \(\pi\) as a blowup of \(N = 9+n\) points in \({\mathbf{P}}^2\). It is known that \(n\leq 10\), c.f. . The image of \(C\) is contained in \({\left\lvert {-2K_{{\mathbf{P}}^2}} \right\rvert} = {\left\lvert {{\mathcal{O}}_{{\mathbf{P}}^2}(6)} \right\rvert}\) and is thus generically a nodal plane sextic where the images of \(C_i\) are its irreducible components.

We will primarily be interested in the case \(n=1\), whence \(S\) is obtained as the blowup of a plane sextic along \(N=10\) ordinary double points, some of which may be infinitely near to each other. Such Coble surfaces are not the image of any birational but not biregular morphism from another Coble surface and are said to be . We say \(S\) is if the blowdown of any \((-1)\)-curve on \(S\) is no longer a Coble surface, or equivalently if \(S\) does not admit a birational but not biregular morphism onto another Coble surface.

Let \(\cL \mathrel{\vcenter{:}}={\mathcal{O}}_S(-K_S) \in \operatorname{Pic}(S)\); By , taking a section \(s\in H^0(\cL ^{\otimes 2})\) with \(Z(s) = C\) yields a double branched double cover \(f: X\to S\) where \(X\) is a smooth K3 surface. By , the preimages \(f^{-1}(C_i)\) are disjoint \((-2)\)-curves and \(\operatorname{Pic}(X)\) is a 2-elementary lattice with invariants of the form \begin{align*} (r,a,\delta)_1 = (10+n, 12-n, \delta)_1 .\end{align*} By , the ramification divisor \(R\) is explicitly of one of the following forms:

  • \(R=\emptyset\) if \((r,a,\delta) = (10, 10, 0)\),
  • \(R\) is a sum of two elliptic curves if \((r,a,\delta) = (10, 8, 0)\),
  • \(R\) is the sum of a single rational curve and \(n-1\) other disjoint \((-2)\)-curves otherwise.

It is also known that \(\delta=1\) unless \(n=8\), c.f. . Thus if \(S\) is a terminal Coble surface of K3 type with \(n=1\), the ramification locus of the K3 cover is a single smooth rational curve, and we obtain a lattice with invariants \begin{align*} S_{\Co} \mathrel{\vcenter{:}}=(11, 11, 1)_1 \cong \left\langle{-2}\right\rangle \oplus E_{10}(2) \end{align*} with orthogonal complement \begin{align*} T_{\Co} \mathrel{\vcenter{:}}= S_{\Co}^{\perp \lkthree} = (11, 11, 1)_2 \cong \rm{I}_{2, 9}(2) \cong \left\langle{2}\right\rangle \oplus E_{10}(2) .\end{align*}

Explicitly, \(S_{\Co}\) is generated by the preimages \(E_i\) for \(1\leq i \leq 10\) under \(f\) of the ten exceptional divisors of the blowup, along with the preimage \(E_0\) under \(f\) of the pullback of a hyperplane class of \({\mathbf{P}}^2\) under \(\pi\). One can show that \(E_0^2=2\) and \(E_i^2 = -2\) for \(i\geq 1\), and thus these divisors form a lattice of the form \begin{align*} S_{\Co} \cong \left\langle{2}\right\rangle \oplus \left\langle{-2}\right\rangle^{\oplus 10} \cong \rm{I}_{1, 10}(2) \cong (11, 11, 1)_1 ,\end{align*} and the identification \(S_{\Co} \cong \left\langle{-2}\right\rangle \oplus E_{10}(2)\) follows from the fact that both definitions of \(S_{\Co}\) are 2-elementary lattices of signature \((1, 10)\) with discriminant group \((\bZ/2\bZ)^{11}\), which are classified uniquely up to isometry by their invariants \((r,a,\delta)_{n_+}\). Similarly, the identification of \(T_{\Co}\) follows from the fact that it is again a 2-elementary lattice of signature \((2, 9)\) satisfying \(q_{T_{\Co}} \cong -q_{S_{\Co}}\), and the isomorphism class of \(q_{T_{\Co}}\) determines \(T_{\Co}\) up to isometry. Alternatively, this can be seen directly using the mirror move \(S\leadsto T\) of applied to \(S_\Co =(11, 11, 1)_1\) in , immediately yielding \(T_{\Co} = (11, 11, 1)_2\).

The lattices \(S_{\Co}\) and \(T_{\Co}\) will be of fundamental importance in constructing the Hodge-theoretic period domain for Coble surfaces, yielding a coarse space for the corresponding moduli space.

Following , we note that this computation is a special case of a general construction. Let \(S\) be any basic rational surface and write \(S\) as the blowup of \({\mathbf{P}}^2\) at \(N\) points \(p_1,\cdots, p_N\) with \(N\geq 9\). It is a fact that \(\operatorname{Pic}(S) \cong \rm{I}_{1, N}\), since one can construct a geometric basis in the following way: let \(e_0\) be the class of the total transform of a hyperplane class in \({\mathbf{P}}^2\) and for \(1\leq i\leq N\), let \(e_i\) be the class of the total transform of the exceptional divisor over \(p_i\). Then \(\operatorname{Pic}(S) = \left\langle{e_0,e_1,\cdots, e_N}\right\rangle\) and \(\rho(X) = N+1\); one verifies that \(e_0^2 = 1\), and for \(i\geq 1\), that \(e_i^2 = -1\). Moreover \(e_ie_j = 0\) for \(i\neq j\), making this an orthogonal basis with respect to the intersection pairing, yielding \(\rm{I}_{1, N}\). In the case of Coble surfaces, the effect of taking the K3 double cover is to twist this lattice by 2, yielding \(\operatorname{Pic}(X) = \rm{I}_{1, N}(2)\), generated by preimages of the \(e_i\). We remark that \begin{align*} K_S = -3e_0 + e_1 + \cdots + e_N .\end{align*}

3.1 Basic Theory

The study of semitoroidal compactifications of moduli spaces of Coble surfaces largely reduces to lattice theory, of which we will now recall the essential notions.

By a lattice, we mean a free \({\mathbf{Z}}\)-module \(L\) of finite rank equipped with a nondegenerate1 symmetric integral bilinear form \(\beta_L: L \otimes_{\mathbf{Z}}L \to {\mathbf{Z}}\). We abbreviate \(vw \mathrel{\vcenter{:}}=\beta_L(v, w)\) and \(v^2 \mathrel{\vcenter{:}}=\beta_L(v, v)\) and refer to the latter as the of \(v\). We write \(L_R \mathrel{\vcenter{:}}= L\otimes_{{\mathbf{Z}}} R\) and \(\beta_{L_R}\) for \(R = {\mathbf{Q}}, {\mathbf{R}}, {\mathbf{C}}\) for the \({\mathbf{Z}}\)-linear extensions of \((L, \beta_L)\) to the rational, real, and complex numbers respectively.

A submodule \(M\subseteq L\) is a sublattice if the restricted bilinear form \({ \left.{{\beta_L}} \right|_{{M}} }\) endows \(M\) with the structure of a lattice. A vector \(v\in L\) is isotropic if \(v^2 = 0\), i.e. it is norm zero, and more generally a sublattice \(M \subseteq L\) is isotropic if \({ \left.{{\beta_L}} \right|_{{M}} } \equiv 0\). A lattice is said to be even if \(x^2\in 2{\mathbf{Z}}\) for all \(x\in L\), and otherwise. A nondegenerate symmetric bilinear form can be linearly extended to \(L_{\mathbf{R}}\) and by Sylvester’s theorem, diagonalized with only \(1\) or \(-1\) on the diagonal. We write \(n_+\) and \(n_-\) respectively for the number of \(\pm 1\) entries on the diagonal. The signature of \(L\) is the pair \((n_+, n_-)\), and the index is \(n_+ - n_-\). We say \(L\) is definite if either \(n_+\) or \(n_-\) is zero, and indefinite otherwise. More precisely, if \(n_- = 0\) we say \(L\) is positive definite, and similarly if \(n_+ = 0\), we say \(L\) is negative definite. A negative definite lattice generated by elements of norm \(-2\) is said to be a root lattice, and such elements are referred to as roots.

The rank \(r\) of a lattice is its rank as a free \({\mathbf{Z}}\)-module and is given by \(r=n_+ + n_- = \dim_{\mathbf{Q}}L_{\mathbf{Q}}\). An indefinite lattice of signature \((1, r-1)\) is said to be hyperbolic. Fixing a generating set \(e_i\) of \(L\), we define the Gram matrix of \(L\) as the matrix \(G_L \mathrel{\vcenter{:}}=(\beta_L(e_i, e_j))_{ij}\), and the discriminant as \(\operatorname{disc} L \mathrel{\vcenter{:}}=\operatorname{det}G_L\). The discriminant is independent of the choice of generating set.

The dual lattice to \(L\) is denoted \(L {}^{ \vee }\mathrel{\vcenter{:}}={ \operatorname{Hom} }_{\mathbf{Z}}(L, {\mathbf{Z}})\), and there is an morphism \begin{align*}\begin{aligned} \iota: L &\hookrightarrow L {}^{ \vee }\\ x &\mapsto \beta_L(x, \cdot) \end{aligned}\end{align*} which, if \(L\) is nondegenerate, is an injection with finite index image. The discriminant group is \(A_L \mathrel{\vcenter{:}}=\operatorname{coker}\iota \cong L {}^{ \vee }/L\); this is a finite order group of order \({\left\lvert {\operatorname{disc} L} \right\rvert}\). We say \(L\) is unimodular if any of the following equivalent conditions hold:

  1. \(A_L\) is the trivial group,

  2. \(\iota\) is an isomorphism and \(L\cong L {}^{ \vee }\),

  3. \({\left\lvert { \operatorname{disc} L} \right\rvert} = 1\).

If \(A_L \cong ({\mathbf{Z}}/p{\mathbf{Z}})^a\) for some \(a\), we say \(L\) is \(p\)-elementary; in our applications we will often have \(p=2\). For even lattices, the form \(\beta_L\) descends to a well-defined quadratic form \begin{align*}\begin{aligned} q_L: A_L &\to {\mathbf{Q}}/2{\mathbf{Z}}\\ x + L &\mapsto \beta_{L_{\mathbf{Q}}}(x, x) \operatorname{mod}2{\mathbf{Z}} \end{aligned}\end{align*} We call the pair \((A_L, q_L)\) the discriminant quadratic form of \(L\). A morphism between two lattices is a morphism of \({\mathbf{Z}}\)-modules \(\eta: L\to L'\) respecting the bilinear forms in the sense that \(\beta_L(x, y) = \beta_{L'}(\eta(x), \eta(y))\), and is a primitive embedding if \(\eta\) is injective and \(\operatorname{coker}\eta\) is torsionfree. An isometry of lattices is an isomorphism, defined in the obvious way. We write \({\operatorname{O}}(L)\) for the group of lattice automorphisms of \(L\), denoted the orthogonal group of \(L\), and similarly \({\operatorname{O}}(q_L)\) for the \({\mathbf{Z}}\)-module automorphisms of of the discriminant form \(A_L\) which preserve the quadratic form. There is a natural group homomorphism \({\operatorname{O}}(L)\to{\operatorname{O}}(q_L)\), the kernel is denoted \(\tilde {\operatorname{O}}(L)\).

Given two lattices \(L_1, L_2\) we write \(L_1\oplus L_2\) for the orthogonal direct sum, which is the direct sum of the underlying modules with bilinear form defined by \begin{align*}\beta_{L_1\oplus L_2}(v_1 + v_2, w_1 + w_2)\mathrel{\vcenter{:}}=\beta_{L_1}(v_1, w_1) + \beta_{L_2}(v_2, w_2) .\end{align*} We write \(L^{\oplus n}\) for the direct sum of \(n\) copies of \(L\). Let \(\eta: M \hookrightarrow L\) be a primitive embedding of lattices, for example the inclusion of a sublattice. We write \begin{align*}M^{\perp L} \mathrel{\vcenter{:}}=\left\{{x\in L \mathrel{\Big|}\beta_L(x, M) = 0}\right\} .\end{align*} If the ambient lattice \(L\) is understood, we often abuse notation and simply write \(M^{\perp}\) without reference to \(L\). Note that \(M^{\perp L}\oplus M \subseteq L\) may not be saturated, and is generally a finite index sublattice of \(L\). We note that \(M^{\perp L}\cap M \neq \left\{{0}\right\}\) in general.2 We also note that for any lattices \(L_i\), \begin{align*} A_{L_1 \oplus \cdots \oplus L_n} = A_{L_1} \oplus \cdots \oplus A_{L_n} \end{align*}

Let \(L\) be a 2-elementary lattice. The divisibility of a vector \(v\in L\), denoted \(\operatorname{div}_L(v)\), is defined by \(\beta_L(v, L) = \operatorname{div}_L(v){\mathbf{Z}}\), i.e. the positive integral generator of the image of the map \(\beta_L(v, \cdot): L\to {\mathbf{Z}}\). For 2-elementary lattices, one always has \(\operatorname{div}_L(v) \in \left\{{1, 2}\right\}\). We set \(v^* \mathrel{\vcenter{:}}= v/\operatorname{div}_L(v)\in A_L\). Letting \(q_L:A_L \to {1\over 2}{\mathbf{Z}}/{\mathbf{Z}}\) be the induced quadratic form on \(A_L\), we say \(v^*\) is characteristic if \(q_L(x) = \beta_L(v^*, x)\operatorname{mod}{\mathbf{Z}}\) for all \(x\in A_L\), and is ordinary otherwise. We say that a primitive isotropic vector \(e\in L\) is

  1. odd if \(\operatorname{div}_L(e) = 1\),

  2. even ordinary if \(\operatorname{div}_L(e) = 2\) and \(e^*\) is ordinary, or

  3. even characteristic if \(\operatorname{div}_L(e) = 2\) and \(e^*\) is characteristic.

The 2-elementary hyperbolic lattices admitting a primitive embedding into \(\Lambda_{\mathrm{K3}}\) were classified by Nikulin in . An indefinite 2-elementary lattice is determined up to isometry by a triple of invariants \((r,a,\delta)\). Here, \(r\mathrel{\vcenter{:}}=\operatorname{rank}_{\mathbf{Z}}(L)\) is the rank, \(a = \operatorname{rank}_{\bF_2}A_L\) is the exponent appearing in \(A_L = ({\mathbf{Z}}/2{\mathbf{Z}})^a\), and \(\delta \in \left\{{0, 1}\right\}\) is the coparity: we set \(\delta = 0\) if \(q_L(A_L) \subseteq {\mathbf{Z}}\), so \(q_L(x) \equiv 0 \operatorname{mod}{\mathbf{Z}}\) for all \(x\in A_L\), and \(\delta=1\) otherwise. We accordingly specify such lattices using the notation \((r,a,\delta)_{n_+}\).

If \(L\) is a lattice with bilinear for \(\beta_L\), define \(L(n)\) to be the twist of \(L\) by \(n\), which has the same underlying \({\mathbf{Z}}\)-module but is equipped with the scaled bilinear form \begin{align*}\beta_{L(n)}(v,w) \mathrel{\vcenter{:}}= n\cdot \beta_L(v, w).\end{align*}

The lattice \(\left\langle{n}\right\rangle\) is defined as the rank 1 lattice \({\mathbf{Z}}\) with one generator \(v\) satisfying \(\beta_{\left\langle{n}\right\rangle}(v,v) = n\). The Gram matrix is the \(1\times 1\) matrix \(G_{\left\langle{n}\right\rangle} = [n]\), and the associated quadratic form is \(q_{\left\langle{n}\right\rangle}(x) = nx^2\).

In rank 2, there are two unimodular hyperbolic lattices: the odd \(\rm{I}_{1, 1} \mathrel{\vcenter{:}}=\left\langle{1}\right\rangle \oplus \left\langle{-1}\right\rangle\), and the even \(U\mathrel{\vcenter{:}}=\rm{II}_{1, 1}\). We refer to the latter as the , which can be realized as \(U \mathrel{\vcenter{:}}={\mathbf{Z}}e \oplus {\mathbf{Z}}f\) with \(e^2=f^2 = 0\) and \(ef = 1\), and thus the following Gram matrix: \begin{align*} G_U = \begin{bmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{bmatrix} .\end{align*}

Any Dynkin diagram of type \(A_n, D_n, E_6, E_7, E_8\) corresponds to a root lattice of the respective type. By convention, we take the negative definite twists of these lattices. Of particular importance to us is the \(E_8\) lattice associated to the following Dynkin diagram:

For any pair of non-negative integers \((p, q)\), there exists an odd indefinite unimodular lattice determined up to isomorphism by its rank and signature: \begin{align*} \mathrm{I}_{p, q} \mathrel{\vcenter{:}}=\left\langle{1}\right\rangle^{\oplus p}\oplus \left\langle{-1}\right\rangle^{\oplus q} .\end{align*}

Let \(L\) be an even indefinite unimodular lattice of signature \((p, q)\). Then \(p-q\equiv 0 \pmod 8\), and \(L\) is uniquely determined up to isomorphism by its rank and signature: \begin{align*} {\rm{II}}_{p, q} \mathrel{\vcenter{:}}= \begin{cases} U ^{\oplus p}\oplus E_8^{\oplus {q-p\over 8}}, & p < q \\ U^{\oplus q} \oplus E_8(-1)^{\oplus {p-q\over 8}}, & p > q .\end{cases} \end{align*}

3.2 Summary

We summarize the lattices that will be relevant to our discussion:

\begin{align*}\begin{aligned} \lkthree &= (22, 0, 0)_3 = U^3 \oplus E_8^2 = \rm{II}_{3, 19} & E_{10} &= (10, 0, 0)_1 = U \oplus E_8 = \mathrm{I}_{1, 9} \\ S_{\operatorname{En}} &= (10, 10, 0)_1 = E_{10}(2) & T_{\operatorname{En}} &= (12, 10, 0)_2 = U \oplus E_{10}(2) \\ S_{\Co} &= (11, 11, 1)_1 = \left\langle{-2}\right\rangle \oplus E_{10}(2) & T_{\Co} &= (11,11,1)_2 = \left\langle{2}\right\rangle \oplus E_{10}(2) \\ S_{\dP} &= (2,2,0)_1 = U(2) & T_{\dP} &= (20, 2, 0)_2 = U \oplus U(2) \oplus E_8^2 \end{aligned}\end{align*}

Writing \[\begin{align*} T_{\Co} = \left\langle{2}\right\rangle \oplus E_{10}(2) &= \langle h, e', f',\alpha_1,\cdots, \alpha_8 \rangle \\ T_{\operatorname{En}} = U \oplus E_{10}(2) &= \langle \tilde e,\tilde f,\tilde e',\tilde f',\tilde \alpha_1,\cdots,\tilde \alpha_8\rangle, \end{align*}\] there is an embedding of lattices \(T_{\Co} \hookrightarrow T_{\operatorname{En}}\): \[\begin{align*} \eta: \left\langle{2}\right\rangle \oplus E_{10}(2) &\to U \oplus E_{10}(2) \\ (h, x) &\mapsto (\tilde e + \tilde f, x) \end{align*}\] which sends the generator \(h\) of \(\left\langle{2}\right\rangle\) to \(\tilde e+\tilde f\in U\) and is the identity on the \(E_{10}(2)\) summand. Since \(\operatorname{coker}\eta\) is torsionfree, \(\eta\) is a primitive embedding.

There is a sequence of primitive embeddings \begin{align*} T_{\Co} \hookrightarrow T_{\operatorname{En}} \hookrightarrow T_{\dP} \hookrightarrow\lkthree \end{align*}
which is unique up to \({\operatorname{O}}(\lkthree)\). In particular, this yields an embedding \(T_{\Co}\hookrightarrow T_{\dP}\) given by \[\begin{align*} \left\langle{2}\right\rangle \oplus U(2) \oplus E_8(2) &\hookrightarrow U \oplus U(2) \oplus E_8^2 \\ (h,x,y) & \mapsto (\tilde e + \tilde f, x, y, y) \end{align*}\] and thus an embedding \(F_{\Co} \hookrightarrow F_{(2,2,0)}\).

We begin with a naive parameter space for Coble surfaces. Following , by varying the coefficients of \(p_i\) in the planar blowup construction, one can construct \(F_\Co\) as a locally closed subvariety of \(({\mathbf{P}}^2)^{10}/\operatorname{PGL}_3\), which is of dimension \begin{align*} \dim ({\mathbf{P}}^2)^{10}/\operatorname{PGL}_3 = \dim ({\mathbf{P}}^2)^{10} - \dim \operatorname{PGL}_3 = 2\cdot 10 - (3^2-1) = 12 .\end{align*} A posteriori, the number of moduli for a Coble surface is 9, which means that there should be 3 conditions imposed upon the configuration of 10 points. Letting \(D\) be the corresponding discriminant locus, we can identify \(F_{\Co}\) as an open subset of \(\qty{ ({\mathbf{P}}^2)^{10} \setminus D }/\operatorname{PGL}_3\) at the level of coarse moduli spaces. We note that shows that \(F_\Co\) is rational by relating it to a codimension one subvariety of a moduli space of certain cuspidal quintics in \({\mathbf{P}}^2\).

Alternatively, Horikawa and more recently consider the following: let \(Y\mathrel{\vcenter{:}}={\mathbf{P}}^1\times {\mathbf{P}}^1\) and define an involution \(\tau(x,y) \mathrel{\vcenter{:}}=(-x,-y)\). The embedding \(\eta: F_{\Co}\to F_{\operatorname{En}}\) induces the correspondence on boundary cusps of the Baily-Borel compactifications shown in .

We prove this correspondence by considering divisibilities at the corresponding 0-cusps and 1-cusps in both moduli spaces. We first note that since \(T_{\Co}\) can be written as \(L(2)\) where \(L = \left\langle{1}\right\rangle \oplus E_8\), every \(v\in T_{\Co}\) satisfies \(\operatorname{div}_{T_{\Co}}(v) = 2\).

Fixing notation, \[\begin{align*} v_1 &\coloneqq e' & w_1 &\coloneqq\eta(v_1) = \tilde e' \\ v_2 &\coloneqq 2h + \alpha_1 + \alpha_2 & w_2 &\coloneqq\eta(v_2) = 2\tilde e + 2\tilde f + \tilde\alpha_1 + \tilde\alpha_2 \\ J &\coloneqq\left\langle{v_1, v_2}\right\rangle & \tilde J &\coloneqq\left\langle{w_1, w_2}\right\rangle \end{align*}\] We then have \(\mathrm{div}_{T_{\operatorname{En}} }(w_1) = 2\).

This follows from the fact that \(\tilde e\in U(2)\) where \(T_{\operatorname{En}} = U \oplus U(2) \oplus E_8(2)\) Noting that \(\tilde e' \tilde f' = 2\), and \(\tilde e'\) is orthogonal to the remaining generators of in the \(U\) and \(E_8(2)\) summands, we thus have \begin{align*} \mathrm{div}_{T_{\operatorname{En}} }(w_1) \mathrel{\vcenter{:}}=\mathrm{div}_{T_{\operatorname{En}} }(\eta(e')) = \mathrm{div}_{T_{\operatorname{En}} }(\tilde e') = 2 .\end{align*} Explicitly, one can check the pairing of \(\tilde e'\) against an arbitrary vector. Write \(x+y+z\in U \oplus U(2)\oplus E_8(2)\), then \begin{align*} \tilde e'\cdot(x+y+z) = \tilde e'\cdot y\in \left\{{0, 2}\right\} \end{align*} since \(y\in U(2)\) and \(\tilde e'\in (U\oplus E_8(2))^{\perp T_{\operatorname{En}}}\). Thus \(\beta_{T_{\operatorname{En}}}(\tilde e', T_{\operatorname{En}}) = \beta_{T_{\operatorname{En}}}(\tilde e', U(2)) = 2\bZ\) since \(\tilde e'\cdot\tilde f' = 2\).
